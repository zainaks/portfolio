Neurodivergency on Campus

A solo heuristic research on understanding the needs of neurodivergent individuals on campus.

Concept

This project was part of a class where I explored photo-elicitation techniques and learned to conduct qualitative interviews to uncover user needs. Although conducted as a conceptual project for a course, this experience taught me valuable skills in user-centered research and data synthesis.

Status

Completed

Timeline

September 2024 - October 2024 (1 month)

Research Goal

Neurodivergent students often face barriers in accessing appropriate resources, which can impact their well-being and academic success. This study seeked to simultaneously explore the challenges faced by neurodivergent students, and analyze the effectiveness of current mental and academic support services already in place for this group in the University of Michigan.

Methodology

Photo-elicitation interviews were selected as the research method to explore the complex and overlapping experiences neurodivergent students encounter between academic responsibilities and campus social life. This method was chosen because visual stimuli (i.e photos) could evoke personalized responses, helping uncover deeper insights into participants' perspectives and experiences.

Digital flyers were distributed, leading to five participants consenting to virtual 45-minute interviews (consent form linked [insert link]). Using a structured interview guide ([insert link]) and a curated set of ten photos ([insert link]) on academic and social life, I prompted participants to reflect on their personal experiences and challenges.

Full interview transcripts were analyzed using a thematic analysis approach. Key patterns and insights were identified by systematically coding participant responses through Google Docs. This process highlighted recurring themes, which were later organized into an affinity diagram to distill actionable findings.

Key Findings

Insights from the thematic analyses of participant’ transcripts were integrated into an affinity diagram, linked below. This visual map organized the data into three overarching themes (key findings), each further subdivided into evidence-based categories. These categories reference original codes, i.e direct quotations, from the interviewees, ensuring that there was direct evidence for each key claim.

Thematic Map (Affinity Diagram)

Participants reported depression and anxiety, worsened by challenges adjusting to the large, overstimulating campus. Several faced difficulties with traditional talk therapy provided by the campus through CAPS, feeling the need to "mask”, despite the therapeutic environment, due to therapists' limited understanding of neurodivergence.

Executive dysfunction, communication barriers, and the absence of supportive communities further compounded their struggles. While the now-defunct Society of Neurodivergent and Disabled Students (SNDS) once provided connection and understanding, its inactivity has left a significant void in campus support.The loss of this group underscores a lack of a very important community on campus, which greatly damages the amount of support that these students truly have.

Reflection

Given limitations of time, and the conceptual nature of this project, the findings are not suitable for statistical analysis due to the sample size and lack of diversity (e.g., all participants were female). However, the study successfully surfaced qualitative insights into how neurodivergent students navigate their environments and highlighted unmet needs in their academic and social lives. These findings provided important insights that can inform future initiatives to improve resources and foster a more supportive campus experience for neurodivergent students.
