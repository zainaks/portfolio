Exploring Mastodon

A user-focused analysis of the usability and effectiveness of a decentralized social media site.

Concept

This two-month project from an introductory information course provided hands-on experience analyzing Mastodon.social’s design, user engagement, and real-world UX concepts like critical mass theory and filter bubbles.

Status

Completed

Timeline

September 2023 - November 2023 (2 months)

Read The Full, In-Depth Analysis

Goal of Analysis

Mastodon.social positions itself as a decentralized social media platform that avoids traditional algorithmic pitfalls and prioritizes user-centric design. This analysis aims to evaluate the validity of these claims and identify potential gaps in usability and user experience.

Methodology

Over two months, I actively used the Mastodon mobile app to observe, analyze, and understand its usability. I documented events, features, and interactions, including trending posts and personal experiences with the interface. To minimize bias, no specific features or issues were targeted initially; instead, usability challenges emerged organically through extended usage.

Key Findings

The most unwavering observation of this study largely fell on the fact that, despite being marketed as “decentralized,” Mastodon’s design mimicked centralized platforms (e.g., its “For You” page). Despite the high potential to promote diverse searching, poorly prioritized features, such as hidden community feeds, further limited user exploration and interaction.

This contradiction, combined with its decentralized algorithm, fostered narrow filter bubbles dominated by politically inclined niches, excluding broader user interests. As a result, Mastodon failed to achieve critical mass, with low user activity and disengagement leading to social loafing, where users lacked the incentive to even interact.

Recommendations

To match it’s algorithm to it’s design, Mastodon can introduce designs that encourage random or underexplored topic feeds, similar to past platforms like Vine, which offered curated content categories on the homepage. The app must also make key features, like said community feed, more immediately visible to reduce cognitive load and foster engagement.

To address its low critical mass, Mastodon could implement tools to support niche community growth, such as a 'newly trending' category tailored to groups receiving low traffic, or with slight alterations to the already existing hashtag system, that could instead promote hashtags from underrepresented groups.

Reflection

This analysis demonstrates the importance of aligning platform design with user behavior and expectations. While Mastodon’s goals were admirable, its execution revealed critical gaps that hindered its user experience. The insights from this project highlight how user engagement and community dynamics are deeply tied to thoughtful algorithmic and design choices.
